# Dialer Pacing and Limits

Outbound execution needs control, not just volume.

Use pacing and campaign limits to decide how quickly a campaign dials, how much live load it can
create, and how much operational pressure the system should absorb at one time.

## What these controls do

In practical terms, teams usually care about two settings first:

- `Calls per second` controls how quickly the dialer starts new calls.
- `Maximum active calls per campaign` controls how many live calls one campaign can have at the
  same time.

Together, these settings help teams:

- control dialer pressure
- protect infrastructure and campaign quality
- adapt pacing to operational needs
- avoid pushing telephony, agent behavior, or support teams too hard too early

## Limit calls per second

Calls per second controls campaign launch pressure.

This setting matters because dialing too aggressively can:

- overload the telephony path
- make campaign behavior harder to interpret
- increase congestion, failed calls, or unstable outcomes
- hide whether the issue belongs to list quality, trunk setup, or workflow behavior

Start conservatively for the first rollout. A low calls-per-second value makes it easier to see how
the system behaves before you push more volume through it.

## Set a maximum number of active calls per campaign

Maximum active calls per campaign controls live concurrency.

This setting matters because it protects the system from building more live call load than the
environment is ready to support. It should be aligned with:

- telephony readiness
- infrastructure capacity
- the support team's ability to monitor live behavior
- how quickly the team can investigate problems if outcomes drift

Use this setting to avoid treating one campaign like an unlimited execution source.

## Campaign execution control in practice

Pacing and concurrency settings directly affect campaign behavior.

When calls per second is too high or the active-call ceiling is too loose, teams may see:

- active calls rising faster than expected
- failed or congestion outcomes increasing
- endpoint or trunk pressure appearing during launch windows
- talk time and ring time trends becoming harder to interpret

When pacing is too low, campaigns may look healthy but move too slowly for the business objective.

The goal is not to maximize throughput immediately. The goal is to reach a controlled operating
point where campaign quality, infrastructure readiness, and operational visibility stay aligned.

## High-concurrency execution

RocketAiFlow has been tested with approximately 300 concurrent calls on an 8 GB machine.

Treat that as a useful internal benchmark example, not as an automatic guarantee for every
environment. Real capacity depends on deployment model, telephony path, codecs, recording and
transcription settings, external actions, and the behavior of the surrounding infrastructure.

Use benchmark examples to frame testing, not to skip validation.

## What to review after launch

After changing pacing or call limits, review:

- active calls versus configured limits
- calls per second or dialing pace
- answered, no answer, busy, failed, and congestion outcomes
- endpoint and trunk state during live dialing
- talk time and ring time trends after the run completes
- call history for individual calls that look unusual

## Best practices

- start with conservative pacing limits
- align campaign limits with infrastructure and telephony readiness
- increase one setting at a time instead of changing pace and concurrency together
- review call history and timing data after each controlled test run
- treat concurrency benchmarks as reference points, not promises
